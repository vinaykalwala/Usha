About PHQ-2

The PHQ-2 (Patient Health Questionnaire-2) is a brief and validated screening tool used to identify potential signs of depression. It consists of 2 core questions that evaluate:

  • Little interest or pleasure in doing things
  • Feeling down, depressed, or hopeless

The goal of the PHQ-2 is not to diagnose depression, but to serve as an initial step in identifying individuals who may benefit from a more in-depth evaluation, such as the PHQ-9.

Using the Calculator: Select your responses to the two questions in the PHQ-2 form above. Based on your score (0 to 6), you will receive a recommendation for whether further assessment is needed.

Scoring Guide:

  • 0–2: Low likelihood of a depressive disorder
  • 3 or higher: Possible depression – further screening (such as PHQ-9) is recommended

If you’re concerned about your mental health, we encourage you to reach out to a licensed mental health professional. Seeking help early can make a meaningful difference.

PHQ-2 Score Statistics

The table below shows the Positive Predictive Value (PPV) and Likelihood Ratio (LR) for different PHQ-2 scores based on research data.

PPV (Positive Predictive Value) indicates the probability that a person actually has a depressive disorder if they score at a certain level. A low PPV (e.g., 15–20%) means that although someone has some symptoms, it's still relatively unlikely they meet the criteria for clinical depression. On the other hand, a high PPV (e.g., above 75%) suggests a strong chance that the person does meet the criteria and should seek further evaluation.

LR (Likelihood Ratio) is a measure used in clinical diagnostics to assess how much a particular test result changes the odds of having a disease. An LR of 1 means no change in risk compared to the general population. A value less than 1 means the score suggests a lower risk of depression, while a value greater than 1 — especially much greater — means a significantly higher risk. For example, an LR of 10 means you're 10 times more likely than average to have the disorder.

These values are presented for two categories:

  • Major Depressive Disorder: A more severe form of depression characterized by persistent low mood, loss of interest, and other serious symptoms.
  • Any Depressive Disorder: This includes both major depression and milder forms, such as dysthymia or minor depressive episodes.

Understanding these statistics helps in identifying how likely someone is to be truly experiencing a depressive condition and whether they should pursue further mental health assessment. Even low scores do not completely rule out emotional distress — they simply suggest a lower clinical likelihood.
