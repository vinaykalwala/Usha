Chemical Exposure Burn Severity Rate Calculator

Note:

Chemical Exposure Burn Severity (CEBS) is an indicative measure used to estimate the potential severity of burns caused by chemical exposure. It takes into account the chemical concentration, duration of exposure, and toxicity level of the substance (measured by LD50). This metric helps assess the level of danger posed by a chemical in a given environment.

Understanding the Parameters:

  • Chemical Concentration (mg/m³ or ppm):
    - This refers to the amount of a chemical present in the air or environment.
    - mg/m³ (milligrams per cubic meter): A measure of how much of a substance is present in a cubic meter of air.
    - ppm (parts per million): A unit of concentration that describes how many parts of a chemical exist per million parts of air.
    - Higher concentrations indicate a greater risk of harmful effects.
  • Exposure Time (minutes or hours):
    - This is the duration for which an individual is exposed to the chemical.
    - Short exposure to highly concentrated chemicals can be as dangerous as long exposure to lower concentrations.
    - The longer the exposure time, the greater the risk of burns or toxic effects.

    Calculation Note: For accuracy, if the exposure time is entered in hours, it is automatically converted to minutes in the calculation (1 hour = 60 minutes). This ensures a consistent formula for determining burn severity.

  • LD50 (Lethal Dose 50%) (mg/kg):
    - LD50 stands for "Lethal Dose for 50% of the population."
    - It represents the dose at which 50% of test subjects (typically animals in laboratory settings) die after exposure.
    - The unit mg/kg means milligrams of substance per kilogram of body weight.
    - A lower LD50 value indicates a more toxic substance, meaning even small amounts can be harmful.

Interpretation Guidelines

While there are no universal thresholds for burn severity, higher CEBS values always indicate greater potential risk. Always compare results against known safety standards for specific chemicals.
