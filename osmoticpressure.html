Reverse osmosis

Reverse osmosis (RO) is a water purification process that uses a semipermeable membrane to remove dissolved salts, contaminants, and impurities from water by applying pressure greater than the natural osmotic pressure. It works by forcing water molecules through the membrane while blocking larger particles, ions, and pollutants, producing clean permeate water and a concentrated waste stream

Explanation of Parameters:

  • Osmotic Coefficient (φ): Corrects for non-ideal behavior in electrolyte solutions (unitless).
  • Ions per Molecule (v): Number of ions dissociated from one electrolyte molecule (e.g., 2 for NaCl).
  • Moles of Electrolyte (n): Amount of dissolved solute in the solution (kmol).
  • Solvent Volume (V): Volume of solvent per kmol (m³/kmol).
  • Temperature (T): Absolute temperature of the solution (K).
  • Osmotic Pressure (Π): Pressure required to prevent solvent flow through a semipermeable membrane (Pa).

Why Osmotic Pressure is Important?

Osmotic pressure helps in designing RO systems, minimizing energy consumption, and preventing membrane fouling by ensuring optimal operating conditions.
